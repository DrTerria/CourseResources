§1–§3 The Algebra of \(\mathbb{C}\)

A complex number is an ordered pair \(z=(x,y)\), written \(z=x+iy\), where \(x=\operatorname{Re}z\) (real part) and \(y=\operatorname{Im}z\) (imaginary part). The symbol \(i\) satisfies \(i^2=-1\). Real numbers are the \(z\) with \(y=0\); pure imaginary numbers have \(x=0\).

Arithmetic \[(x_1+iy_1)+(x_2+iy_2)=(x_1+x_2)+i(y_1+y_2)\] \[(x_1+iy_1)(x_2+iy_2)=(x_1x_2-y_1y_2)+i(x_1y_2+x_2y_1)\] Just multiply normally and replace \(i^2\) by \(-1\).

\(\mathbb{C}\) is a field: addition and multiplication are commutative and associative, multiplication distributes over addition, \(0=(0,0)\) and \(1=(1,0)\) are the identities, every \(z\) has an additive inverse \(-z\), and every \(z\neq0\) has a multiplicative inverse. There is no ordering "\(<\)" on \(\mathbb{C}\) (inequalities only ever apply to real quantities such as \(|z|\)).

Inverse & division \[z^{-1}=\frac{x-iy}{x^2+y^2}\qquad(z\neq0)\] \[\frac{z_1}{z_2}=\frac{z_1\,\overline{z_2}}{z_2\,\overline{z_2}}=\frac{(x_1+iy_1)(x_2-iy_2)}{x_2^2+y_2^2}\] To divide: multiply top and bottom by the conjugate of the denominator.

Problem patterns

HW pp. 4–5, 7–8 Verify field identities; reduce expressions like \(\dfrac{1}{1-i}\) or \(\dfrac{5}{(1-i)(2-i)(3-i)}\) to the form \(x+iy\); prove the binomial-type and algebraic identities (e.g. \((1+z)^2=1+2z+z^2\), or that \(z^2=\bar z^2\iff z\) is real or pure imaginary). Standard moves: clear denominators by conjugates; separate real/imaginary parts.

Worked. \(\dfrac{1}{1-i}=\dfrac{1+i}{(1-i)(1+i)}=\dfrac{1+i}{2}=\tfrac12+\tfrac12 i.\)

§4–§5 Vectors, Moduli, and the Triangle Inequality

Identify \(z=x+iy\) with the vector/point \((x,y)\). Addition is vector addition (parallelogram rule).

Modulus \[|z|=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\ge 0,\qquad |z|^2=x^2+y^2=z\bar z.\] \(|z|\) is the distance from \(0\) to \(z\); \(|z_1-z_2|\) is the distance between two points. So \(|z-z_0|=R\) is a circle of radius \(R\) about \(z_0\), and \(|z-z_0|\le R\) is a closed disk.
Modulus rules & triangle inequalities \[|z_1z_2|=|z_1||z_2|,\qquad \left|\tfrac{z_1}{z_2}\right|=\tfrac{|z_1|}{|z_2|}.\] \[|z_1+z_2|\le |z_1|+|z_2|\quad(\text{triangle inequality})\] \[\big|\,|z_1|-|z_2|\,\big|\le |z_1\pm z_2|\quad(\text{reverse triangle inequality})\] Useful forms: \(\operatorname{Re}z\le|\operatorname{Re}z|\le|z|\), \(\ \operatorname{Im}z\le|z|\).
Why the reverse inequality matters For the integral bounds in §47 you constantly need a lower bound on a denominator. The key tool is \[|z^n + a|\ \ge\ \big|\,|z|^n-|a|\,\big|.\] E.g. on \(|z|=2\): \(|z^4+1|\ge |2^4-1|=15\). Memorize this — it appears on the exam.

Problem patterns

HW pp. 13–14, 16–17 Locate/sketch sets defined by modulus conditions (\(|z-1+i|=1\) is a circle centered \(1-i\)); prove inequalities using triangle/reverse-triangle; show \(\sqrt2\,|z|\ge|\operatorname{Re}z|+|\operatorname{Im}z|\); establish algebraic identities like \(|z_1z_2|=|z_1||z_2|\) and \(|z_1-z_2|^2=|z_1|^2-2\operatorname{Re}(z_1\bar z_2)+|z_2|^2\).

§6 Complex Conjugates

Conjugate \[\bar z = x-iy,\qquad \overline{\bar z}=z,\qquad |\bar z|=|z|,\qquad z\bar z=|z|^2.\] \[\overline{z_1\pm z_2}=\bar z_1\pm\bar z_2,\quad \overline{z_1z_2}=\bar z_1\,\bar z_2,\quad \overline{(z_1/z_2)}=\bar z_1/\bar z_2.\] \[\operatorname{Re}z=\frac{z+\bar z}{2},\qquad \operatorname{Im}z=\frac{z-\bar z}{2i}.\]

Conjugation reflects across the real axis. A number is real iff \(z=\bar z\) and pure imaginary iff \(z=-\bar z\).

HW pp. 16–17 Use \(z\bar z=|z|^2\) and conjugate rules to simplify, prove \(\overline{z^2}=\bar z^{\,2}\), or rewrite modulus identities. The trick "\(|z|^2=z\bar z\)" converts modulus problems into algebra.

§7–§9 Exponential Form & Arguments

Polar / exponential form With \(r=|z|\) and \(\theta=\arg z\): \[z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)=re^{i\theta}\qquad(\text{Euler: } e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta).\] \(\theta\) is the argument, defined only up to multiples of \(2\pi\). The principal value \(\Theta=\operatorname{Arg}z\) is the one with \(-\pi<\Theta\le\pi\), and \(\arg z=\Theta+2n\pi\ (n\in\mathbb{Z}).\)
Multiplication, powers, de Moivre \[z_1z_2=r_1r_2\,e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)},\qquad \frac{z_1}{z_2}=\frac{r_1}{r_2}e^{i(\theta_1-\theta_2)}.\] \[z^n=r^n e^{in\theta},\qquad (\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^n=\cos n\theta+i\sin n\theta.\] \[\arg(z_1z_2)=\arg z_1+\arg z_2,\qquad \arg(z_1/z_2)=\arg z_1-\arg z_2\] (as equalities between sets of values — they need NOT hold for the principal value \(\operatorname{Arg}\)).
Common trap \(\operatorname{Arg}(z_1z_2)\neq \operatorname{Arg}z_1+\operatorname{Arg}z_2\) in general — the sum can fall outside \((-\pi,\pi]\) and must be adjusted by \(\pm2\pi\). The identity is true only for the multivalued \(\arg\).
HW pp. 23–25 Put numbers in exponential form (e.g. \(-1-\sqrt3\,i = 2e^{-i2\pi/3}\)); use de Moivre to derive \(\cos3\theta,\sin3\theta\); compute powers like \((1+i)^{n}\); verify \(\arg\) relations and find \(\operatorname{Arg}\) of products/quotients.

Worked. \((1+i)^{10}\): write \(1+i=\sqrt2\,e^{i\pi/4}\). Then \((1+i)^{10}=(\sqrt2)^{10}e^{i10\pi/4}=2^{5}e^{i5\pi/2}=32\,e^{i\pi/2}=32i.\)

§10–§11 Roots of Complex Numbers

\(n\)th roots The \(n\) distinct \(n\)th roots of \(z_0=r_0e^{i\theta_0}\) are \[c_k=\sqrt[n]{r_0}\,\exp\!\Big[i\Big(\frac{\theta_0+2k\pi}{n}\Big)\Big],\qquad k=0,1,\dots,n-1.\] They lie on the circle \(|z|=\sqrt[n]{r_0}\), equally spaced by angle \(2\pi/n\). The principal root uses \(\theta_0=\operatorname{Arg}z_0\) and \(k=0\).

The \(n\)th roots of unity are \(1,\omega,\omega^2,\dots,\omega^{n-1}\) where \(\omega=e^{i2\pi/n}\); they form a regular \(n\)-gon and sum to \(0\).

HW pp. 30–32, 34–35 Find all \(n\)th roots and plot them (e.g. the three cube roots of \(-8i\), the square roots of \(i\)); express roots in \(x+iy\) form; verify the roots-of-unity sum is zero. Method: convert to \(re^{i\theta}\), apply the formula, step \(k=0,\dots,n-1\).

Worked. Square roots of \(i=e^{i\pi/2}\): \(c_k=e^{i(\pi/2+2k\pi)/2}\), \(k=0,1\Rightarrow e^{i\pi/4}=\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}(1+i)\) and \(e^{i5\pi/4}=-\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}(1+i).\)

§13–§14 Functions and Mappings

A complex function \(w=f(z)\) assigns to each \(z=x+iy\) in a domain a value \(w=u+iv\). Writing

\[f(z)=u(x,y)+i\,v(x,y),\]

splits \(f\) into two real-valued functions of two real variables. (In polar form, \(f=u(r,\theta)+iv(r,\theta).\)) Because we can't graph \(w\) vs. \(z\) in 2-D, we picture \(f\) as a mapping from a \(z\)-plane to a \(w\)-plane: it sends curves and regions to image curves and regions.

The map \(w=z^2\) With \(z=re^{i\theta}\), \(w=r^2e^{i2\theta}\): moduli square, angles double. In rectangular form \(u=x^2-y^2,\ v=2xy\). It maps the hyperbolas \(x^2-y^2=c_1\) and \(2xy=c_2\) to vertical/horizontal lines \(u=c_1,\ v=c_2\), and the upper half plane onto the whole plane.

HW pp. 43–44 Write \(f\) as \(u+iv\); describe images of lines, rays, circles, or regions under \(w=z^2\), \(w=1/z\), translations and rotations. Strategy: use polar form for power maps, rectangular form for linear maps; track what happens to \(r,\theta\) or to \(x,y\).

§15–§18 Limits and Continuity

Limit \(\displaystyle\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=w_0\) means: for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(\delta>0\) so that \[0<|z-z_0|<\delta\ \Rightarrow\ |f(z)-w_0|<\varepsilon.\] The limit, if it exists, is unique, and \(z\) may approach \(z_0\) from any direction. If two different paths give different values, the limit does not exist.
Real/imaginary split (Thm, §15–16) With \(f=u+iv\), \(z_0=x_0+iy_0\), \(w_0=u_0+iv_0\): \[\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=w_0\iff \lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)}u=u_0\ \text{ and }\ \lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)}v=v_0.\]
Limit laws (§16) If \(\lim f=W\) and \(\lim g=W'\) (as \(z\to z_0\)) then the limit of a sum, product, and quotient (denominator \(\neq0\)) is \(W+W'\), \(WW'\), \(W/W'\). Polynomials behave like in calculus.
Point at infinity (§17) On the Riemann sphere, the point \(\infty\) is added. Useful equivalences: \[\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=\infty \iff \lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{1}{f(z)}=0,\] \[\lim_{z\to\infty}f(z)=w_0 \iff \lim_{z\to0}f(1/z)=w_0,\] \[\lim_{z\to\infty}f(z)=\infty \iff \lim_{z\to0}\frac{1}{f(1/z)}=0.\]

Continuity (§18): \(f\) is continuous at \(z_0\) if \(f(z_0)\) exists, \(\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)\) exists, and the two are equal. Sums, products, quotients, and compositions of continuous functions are continuous. A function continuous on a closed bounded region is bounded there and \(|f|\) attains a maximum — this fact is the backbone of §47.

HW pp. 54–55 Compute limits using the laws; show a limit fails to exist by comparing two paths (classic: \(\lim_{z\to0}\bar z/z\) gives \(1\) along the real axis, \(-1\) along the imaginary axis ⟹ DNE); evaluate limits involving \(\infty\) via the equivalences above.

§19–§20 Derivatives

Derivative \[f'(z_0)=\lim_{\Delta z\to0}\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z},\] provided the limit exists. As with limits, \(\Delta z\to0\) from every direction must give the same value — this is a far stronger requirement than real differentiability.

All the familiar rules hold (same proofs):

\[\tfrac{d}{dz}c=0,\quad \tfrac{d}{dz}z^n=nz^{n-1},\quad (f\pm g)'=f'\pm g',\] \[(fg)'=f'g+fg',\quad \Big(\tfrac{f}{g}\Big)'=\frac{f'g-fg'}{g^2},\quad \tfrac{d}{dz}f(g(z))=f'(g(z))g'(z).\]
Common trap — \(f(z)=\bar z\) \(f(z)=\bar z\) is continuous everywhere but differentiable nowhere. The difference quotient \(\overline{\Delta z}/\Delta z\) equals \(1\) along the real direction and \(-1\) along the imaginary direction. Likewise \(|z|^2\) is differentiable only at \(z=0\). Existence of \(f'\) is special.
HW pp. 61–62 Compute derivatives via the rules; use the definition to show \(\bar z\), \(\operatorname{Re}z\), \(|z|^2\) are non-differentiable (away from special points); apply chain/quotient rules.

§21–§24 The Cauchy–Riemann Equations

C–R equations (necessary, §21) If \(f=u+iv\) is differentiable at \(z_0=x_0+iy_0\), then \(u,v\) have first-order partials there satisfying \[\boxed{\,u_x=v_y\quad\text{and}\quad u_y=-v_x\,}\] and \(\displaystyle f'(z_0)=u_x+iv_x = v_y - i\,u_y.\)
Sufficient conditions (§23) If \(u,v\) and their first partials are continuous in a neighborhood of \(z_0\) and satisfy the C–R equations at \(z_0\), then \(f'(z_0)\) exists. (C–R alone is necessary but not sufficient; continuity of the partials is what closes the gap.)
Polar form (§24) With \(z=re^{i\theta}\), \(f=u(r,\theta)+iv(r,\theta)\), the C–R equations become \[r\,u_r=v_\theta,\qquad u_\theta=-r\,v_r\quad(r>0),\] \[f'(z_0)=e^{-i\theta}\big(u_r+i\,v_r\big).\]
Common trap C–R holding at a point does not make \(f\) differentiable there unless the partials are also continuous nearby. And differentiability at one point does not imply analyticity (next section). Order matters: necessary vs. sufficient.
HW pp. 70–72 Verify or apply C–R to decide where \(f'\) exists and compute it; use the polar C–R for functions natural in \(r,\theta\); show where functions like \(f(z)=e^x e^{-iy}\) (i.e. \(e^{\bar z}\)) fail to be differentiable.

Worked. \(f(z)=z^2=x^2-y^2+i(2xy)\): \(u_x=2x=v_y\), \(u_y=-2y=-v_x\) ✓ everywhere, partials continuous ⟹ differentiable everywhere with \(f'=u_x+iv_x=2x+i2y=2z.\)

§25–§26 Analytic Functions

Definition \(f\) is analytic (holomorphic) at \(z_0\) if it is differentiable at every point of some neighborhood of \(z_0\). \(f\) is analytic in a domain \(D\) if differentiable at every point of \(D\). An entire function is analytic on all of \(\mathbb{C}\) (e.g. polynomials, \(e^z\)).

"Analytic at a point" is therefore a neighborhood property, strictly stronger than "differentiable at a point." A singular point (singularity) \(z_0\) is a point where \(f\) fails to be analytic but is analytic at some point in every neighborhood of \(z_0\).

Key consequence If \(f'(z)=0\) everywhere in a domain \(D\), then \(f\) is constant on \(D\). (Used repeatedly later.)
HW pp. 76–77 Determine domains of analyticity; locate singular points (e.g. \(f=1/(z^2+1)\) singular at \(\pm i\)); show a function with \(f'\equiv0\) is constant; argue why a function differentiable only on a curve/point is nowhere analytic.

§27 Harmonic Functions

Harmonic & harmonic conjugate A real function \(H(x,y)\) is harmonic in a domain if it has continuous 2nd partials and satisfies Laplace's equation \[H_{xx}+H_{yy}=0.\] Theorem. If \(f=u+iv\) is analytic in \(D\), then \(u\) and \(v\) are each harmonic in \(D\). Here \(v\) is a harmonic conjugate of \(u\) (order matters — \(-u\) is a harmonic conjugate of \(v\)).

To build a harmonic conjugate \(v\) of a given harmonic \(u\): integrate the C–R equations. From \(v_y=u_x\) integrate in \(y\), then differentiate in \(x\) and match \(v_x=-u_y\) to pin down the "constant" of integration.

HW pp. 89–90 Show a given \(u\) is harmonic; find its harmonic conjugate \(v\) and the analytic \(f=u+iv\); verify the conjugate relationship is not symmetric. Watch the sign convention in \(u_y=-v_x\).

Worked. \(u=x^2-y^2\): \(u_x=2x\Rightarrow v_y=2x\Rightarrow v=2xy+\phi(x)\). Then \(v_x=2y+\phi'(x)\) must equal \(-u_y=2y\), so \(\phi'(x)=0\), \(\phi=c\). Thus \(v=2xy+c\) and \(f=z^2+ic.\)

§28–§29 Uniquely Determined Analytic Functions & the Reflection Principle

Uniqueness (§28): if two functions are analytic in a domain \(D\) and agree on a line segment (or any set with a limit point) inside \(D\), they agree throughout \(D\). Equivalently, an analytic function that is \(0\) along a segment in \(D\) is identically \(0\). This is why analytic functions are extraordinarily "rigid."

Reflection principle (§29): Suppose \(f\) is analytic in a domain \(D\) symmetric about the real axis. Then \[\overline{f(\bar z)}=f(z)\ \text{ for all }z\in D \iff f(x)\ \text{is real on the segment of the real axis in } D.\] So a function real on a real interval satisfies \(\overline{f(\bar z)}=f(z)\).

HW pp. 95–97, 99–100 Apply uniqueness to show identities extend from \(\mathbb R\) to \(\mathbb C\); verify the reflection identity for entire functions like \(z^3\), \(e^z\); explain why it fails when \(f\) isn't real on the axis (e.g. \(iz+1\)).

§30 The Exponential Function

Definition & properties \[e^z=e^{x}(\cos y+i\sin y)\qquad(z=x+iy).\] So \(|e^z|=e^x\) and \(\arg(e^z)=y\,(+2n\pi)\). Key facts: \[e^{z_1}e^{z_2}=e^{z_1+z_2},\quad \frac{e^{z_1}}{e^{z_2}}=e^{z_1-z_2},\quad (e^z)'=e^z\ (\text{entire}).\] \(e^z\) is never zero (\(|e^z|=e^x>0\)) and is periodic with period \(2\pi i\): \(e^{z+2\pi i}=e^z\).
TrapUnlike the real case, \(e^z\) is not one-to-one and \(e^z<0\) is possible? No — \(e^z\neq0\), but \(e^z\) can be any nonzero complex number, e.g. \(e^{i\pi}=-1\). Also \(e^{z_1}=e^{z_2}\iff z_1-z_2=2n\pi i\).
HW p. 103 Compute \(e^z\) in \(x+iy\) form; solve \(e^z=\) given value (find all \(z\)); show \(|e^{z^2}|\le e^{|z|^2}\); verify periodicity and the modulus/argument identities.

§31–§34 The Logarithmic Function

Logarithm (multivalued) \[\log z=\ln|z|+i\arg z=\ln r + i(\Theta+2n\pi),\quad n\in\mathbb Z,\ z\neq0,\] where \(\Theta=\operatorname{Arg}z\). It is the inverse of \(e^z\): \(e^{\log z}=z\), and \(\log(e^z)=z+2n\pi i\).
Principal value & branches \[\operatorname{Log}z=\ln r + i\Theta\qquad(-\pi<\Theta\le\pi).\] A branch fixes \(\arg z\) in an interval \(\alpha<\theta<\alpha+2\pi\), making \(\log\) single-valued and analytic on the plane minus a branch cut (a ray from the origin). For the principal branch the cut is the negative real axis including \(0\); there \[\frac{d}{dz}\operatorname{Log}z=\frac1z\quad(|z|>0,\ -\pi<\operatorname{Arg}z<\pi).\] The point \(0\) is a branch point.
Log identities (§34) As multivalued sets: \[\log(z_1z_2)=\log z_1+\log z_2,\qquad \log(z_1/z_2)=\log z_1-\log z_2.\] These can fail for \(\operatorname{Log}\) (principal) by a \(\pm2\pi i\) correction. Also \(\log(z^n)\neq n\log z\) in general.
HW pp. 107–109, 111–112 Evaluate \(\log\) and \(\operatorname{Log}\) of specific numbers (e.g. \(\operatorname{Log}(-ei)=1-i\pi/2\)); solve \(\log z = \) value; show identity failures with explicit examples; identify domains where a chosen branch is analytic.

Worked. \(\log(-1)=\ln1+i(\pi+2n\pi)=(2n+1)\pi i\); principal value \(\operatorname{Log}(-1)=i\pi.\)

§35–§36 The Power Function

Complex powers For \(z\neq0\) and any complex \(c\), \[z^{c}=e^{\,c\log z}\quad(\text{multivalued}),\qquad \text{P.V. } z^{c}=e^{\,c\operatorname{Log}z}.\] On a branch of \(\log\), \(z^c\) is analytic with \(\dfrac{d}{dz}z^c = c\,z^{c-1}.\) The exponential base form \(c^z=e^{z\log c}\) is also defined this way.
TrapPowers are generally multivalued; e.g. \(i^{i}=e^{i\log i}=e^{i\cdot i(\pi/2+2n\pi)}=e^{-(\pi/2+2n\pi)}\) — infinitely many real values; principal value \(e^{-\pi/2}\).
HW pp. 111–112, 119–120 Compute all values (and the P.V.) of powers like \((1+i)^{i}\), \((-1)^{1/\pi}\), \(i^{i}\); differentiate branch-defined powers; show when \(z^cz^d=z^{c+d}\) holds.

§37–§38 Trigonometric Functions

Definitions \[\sin z=\frac{e^{iz}-e^{-iz}}{2i},\qquad \cos z=\frac{e^{iz}+e^{-iz}}{2}.\] Both are entire with \((\sin z)'=\cos z\), \((\cos z)'=-\sin z\); all the usual identities (\(\sin^2z+\cos^2z=1\), addition formulas, periodicity \(2\pi\)) carry over.
Real/imaginary parts & moduli \[\sin z=\sin x\cosh y+i\cos x\sinh y,\quad \cos z=\cos x\cosh y - i\sin x\sinh y,\] \[|\sin z|^2=\sin^2x+\sinh^2y,\qquad |\cos z|^2=\cos^2x+\sinh^2y.\]
Big difference from real trig \(\sin z\) and \(\cos z\) are unbounded (because \(\sinh y\to\infty\)). Their only zeros are the real ones: \(\sin z=0\iff z=n\pi\); \(\cos z=0\iff z=\tfrac\pi2+n\pi\). The other functions \(\tan,\cot,\sec,\csc\) are defined as ratios and are analytic except where their denominators vanish.
HW pp. 133–135, 138–140 Verify identities; compute \(|\sin z|^2\); locate all zeros of \(\sin,\cos\); solve equations like \(\cos z=2\) (note: possible in \(\mathbb C\)!); show unboundedness.

§39 Hyperbolic Functions

Definitions & links to trig \[\sinh z=\frac{e^{z}-e^{-z}}{2},\qquad \cosh z=\frac{e^{z}+e^{-z}}{2},\] \[(\sinh z)'=\cosh z,\quad (\cosh z)'=\sinh z,\quad \cosh^2z-\sinh^2z=1.\] Bridges to trig: \[\sinh(iz)=i\sin z,\quad \cosh(iz)=\cos z,\quad \sin(iz)=i\sinh z,\quad \cos(iz)=\cosh z.\] Zeros: \(\sinh z=0\iff z=n\pi i\); \(\cosh z=0\iff z=(\tfrac\pi2+n\pi)i.\)
HW p. 147 Prove hyperbolic identities; relate to trig via the \(iz\) substitutions; find zeros; compute real/imaginary parts.

§40 Inverse Trigonometric and Hyperbolic Functions

Solving e.g. \(z=\sin w\) leads to a quadratic in \(e^{iw}\); the inverses are therefore multivalued and expressible through logs and square roots:

Key formulas \[\sin^{-1}z=-i\log\!\big(iz+(1-z^2)^{1/2}\big),\qquad \cos^{-1}z=-i\log\!\big(z+i(1-z^2)^{1/2}\big),\] \[\tan^{-1}z=\frac{i}{2}\log\frac{i+z}{i-z}.\] Differentiating (on a branch): \(\dfrac{d}{dz}\sin^{-1}z=\dfrac{1}{(1-z^2)^{1/2}}\), \(\ \dfrac{d}{dz}\tan^{-1}z=\dfrac{1}{1+z^2}.\) Analogous log-formulas hold for \(\sinh^{-1},\cosh^{-1},\tanh^{-1}\).
HW pp. 159–162 Derive these formulas; find all values of \(\sin^{-1}(\text{number})\), \(\cos^{-1}2\), etc.; differentiate. Remember: multivaluedness comes from both the \(\log\) and the square root.

§41–§42 Derivatives and Integrals of \(w(t)\)

Here \(w(t)=u(t)+iv(t)\) is a complex-valued function of a real variable \(t\in[a,b]\). Differentiation and integration are done componentwise:

Calculus of \(w(t)\) \[w'(t)=u'(t)+i\,v'(t),\qquad \int_a^b w(t)\,dt=\int_a^b u\,dt + i\int_a^b v\,dt.\] Linearity holds. The Fundamental Theorem holds: if \(W'(t)=w(t)\) then \(\int_a^b w\,dt = W(b)-W(a).\) Properties (used in §47): \[\operatorname{Re}\int_a^b w\,dt=\int_a^b \operatorname{Re}\,w\,dt,\qquad \operatorname{Im}\int_a^b w\,dt=\int_a^b \operatorname{Im}\,w\,dt.\]
TrapThe Mean Value Theorem for derivatives fails for \(w(t)\) (e.g. \(w=e^{it}\) on \([0,2\pi]\)). Many real-calculus shortcuts don't transfer; integrate/differentiate parts separately.
HW pp. 170–172 Evaluate \(\int_a^b w(t)\,dt\) for things like \(e^{(1+i)t}\), \((1+it)^2\); use the FTC for \(w(t)\); demonstrate the failure of the MVT.

Worked. \(\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/4} e^{it}\,dt=\Big[\tfrac{1}{i}e^{it}\Big]_0^{\pi/4}=\tfrac1i\big(e^{i\pi/4}-1\big)= \tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}+i\big(1-\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\big).\)

Terminology for the curves we integrate over:

The Jordan curve theorem: a simple closed contour divides the plane into an inside (bounded) and outside. Positive orientation = counterclockwise.

§44–§46 Contour Integrals

Definition For \(f\) piecewise continuous on a contour \(C:z=z(t),\ a\le t\le b\), \[\int_C f(z)\,dz=\int_a^b f\big(z(t)\big)\,z'(t)\,dt.\] The right side is an ordinary \(w(t)\)-integral.
Properties \[\int_C \big(k_1f+k_2g\big)\,dz=k_1\!\int_C f\,dz+k_2\!\int_C g\,dz,\] \[\int_{-C} f\,dz=-\int_{C} f\,dz\quad(\text{reverse orientation}),\qquad \int_{C_1+C_2}=\int_{C_1}+\int_{C_2}.\] The value is independent of parametrization (within a fixed orientation), but in general does depend on the path \(C\).
Two fundamental computations to memorize Around the positively oriented circle \(C:|z-z_0|=R\) (use \(z=z_0+Re^{i\theta},\ z'=iRe^{i\theta}\)): \[\int_{C}\frac{dz}{z-z_0}=2\pi i,\qquad \int_{C}(z-z_0)^{\,n-1}dz=\begin{cases}2\pi i,& n=0\\[2pt]0,& n=\pm1,\pm2,\dots\end{cases}\] The first is THE most important integral in the course.

Branch cuts (§46): when the integrand involves a multivalued function (\(\log\), \(z^{a}\)), fix a branch first so the integrand is continuous along \(C\), and make sure \(C\) avoids the branch cut.

HW pp. 195–197, 205–208 Evaluate \(\int_C f\,dz\) by direct parametrization for \(f=\bar z,\ 1/z,\ z^n,\ e^z,\ \) etc. over segments, circles, and arcs; show path-dependence by comparing two contours with the same endpoints; handle a branch-cut example (e.g. \(\int_C z^{1/2}dz\) on a chosen branch).

Worked (path dependence). \(\int_C \bar z\,dz\) from \(0\) to \(1+i\): along the straight segment \(z=t(1+i)\), \(dz=(1+i)dt\), \(\bar z=t(1-i)\): integral \(=\int_0^1 t(1-i)(1+i)\,dt=\int_0^1 2t\,dt=1\). Along the path \(0\to1\to1+i\) you get a different value — so \(\int\bar z\,dz\) is path-dependent (\(\bar z\) is not analytic).

§47 Upper Bounds for Moduli of Contour Integrals EXAM FOCUS

Lemma For a piecewise-continuous \(w(t)\) on \([a,b]\): \[\left|\int_a^b w(t)\,dt\right|\le \int_a^b |w(t)|\,dt.\] (Proof idea: write the left integral as \(r_0e^{i\theta_0}\), so \(r_0=\int_a^b\operatorname{Re}[e^{-i\theta_0}w(t)]\,dt\le\int_a^b|w(t)|\,dt\), using \(\operatorname{Re}\zeta\le|\zeta|\).)
★ The ML Inequality (the theorem) Let \(C\) be a contour of length \(L\), and \(f\) piecewise continuous on \(C\). If \[|f(z)|\le M\quad\text{for all }z\text{ on }C,\] then \[\boxed{\ \left|\int_C f(z)\,dz\right|\le M\,L.\ }\] Such an \(M\) always exists when \(f\) is continuous on \(C\) (a continuous function on a closed bounded set attains a max).
Strategy — this is exactly the exam skill To bound \(\big|\int_C f\,dz\big|\):
  1. Find \(L\), the length of \(C\) (e.g. a circular arc of radius \(R\) subtending angle \(\alpha\) has \(L=\alpha R\); a full circle \(L=2\pi R\); a semicircle \(L=\pi R\)).
  2. Bound \(|f(z)|\le M\) on \(C\). Bound the numerator above with the triangle inequality \(|a+b|\le|a|+|b|\), and the denominator below with the reverse triangle inequality \(|z^n+a|\ge\big|\,|z|^n-|a|\,\big|\). On \(|z|=R\), \(|z|=R\) is constant, which makes this clean.
  3. Conclude \(\big|\int_C f\,dz\big|\le ML\). For limits as \(R\to\infty\), show \(M(R)\,L(R)\to0\).

Worked (Example 1, §47). \(C\): arc of \(|z|=2\) in the first quadrant, from \(2\) to \(2i\), so \(L=\tfrac14(2\pi\cdot2)=\pi\). For \(z\) on \(C\): \(|z-2|\le|z|+2=4\) and \(|z^4+1|\ge|z|^4-1=15\). Hence \(\Big|\dfrac{z-2}{z^4+1}\Big|\le\dfrac{4}{15}=M\), giving \[\left|\int_C\frac{z-2}{z^4+1}\,dz\right|\le \frac{4}{15}\cdot\pi=\frac{4\pi}{15}.\]

Worked (Example 2 — vanishing limit). \(C_R\): semicircle \(z=Re^{i\theta}\), \(0\le\theta\le\pi\), \(R>3\), \(L=\pi R\). On \(C_R\): \(|z+1|\le R+1\), \(|z^2+4|\ge R^2-4\), \(|z^2+9|\ge R^2-9\). So \[\left|\int_{C_R}\frac{(z+1)\,dz}{(z^2+4)(z^2+9)}\right|\le \frac{(R+1)\pi R}{(R^2-4)(R^2-9)}\xrightarrow[R\to\infty]{}0,\] since the bound behaves like \(\pi R^2/R^4=\pi/R^2\to0\). Therefore the integral \(\to0\) without ever evaluating it.

HW pp. 218–221 Pure ML-inequality problems: bound integrals over arcs/circles (\(\big|\int_C \tfrac{z+4}{z^3-1}dz\big|\le \tfrac{6\pi}{7}\) on the §47 arc; \(\big|\int_C \tfrac{dz}{z^4}\big|\le 4\sqrt2\) on the segment from \(i\) to \(1\) using the nearest-point distance \(d=\sqrt2/2\)); show triangle-inequality bounds (\(|e^z-\bar z|\le e^x+\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\)); and prove \(R\to\infty\) (or \(\rho\to0\)) limits vanish by dividing numerator/denominator by the top power of \(R\). These problems are the most likely exam questions from this section.

Top exam-day reminders
  • Limits/derivatives must be path-independent — show DNE by two paths (\(\bar z/z\)).
  • C–R is necessary; need continuous partials for sufficient. Differentiable ≠ analytic.
  • \(\operatorname{Arg}\), \(\operatorname{Log}\), principal powers can break the "additive" identities by \(\pm2\pi(i)\).
  • \(e^z\) is never zero; \(\sin,\cos\) are unbounded and have only real zeros.
  • Contour integrals are generally path-dependent unless the integrand is analytic.
  • Memorize \(\int_C dz/(z-z_0)=2\pi i\) and the ML inequality cold.
