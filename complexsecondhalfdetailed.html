Complex Variables — The Second Half

A ground-up study guide · Cauchy's theorems, series, and residues

This guide teaches the second half of the course from zero. It assumes you can compute contour integrals by parametrization and use the ML inequality (the first-half material), and builds everything after that: why analytic functions make integrals collapse, how series expansions encode a function's behavior near its bad points, and how residues turn hard integrals into bookkeeping. Concepts come with the why, common traps are flagged, and "instructor extras" like the winding number get their own section.

The arc of the second half — read this first The whole story is one escalating question: when is \(\oint_C f\,dz\) easy?
  1. §48: If \(f\) has an antiderivative, closed loops give \(0\) and open paths only need endpoints.
  2. §50: Even without finding an antiderivative, analyticity inside and on the loop forces \(\oint=0\) (Cauchy–Goursat).
  3. §54: If there's exactly one bad point inside, the integral isn't \(0\) — it's \(2\pi i\) times the value (or derivative) of the nice part at that point (Cauchy integral formula).
  4. §62–72: Series (Taylor, Laurent) give us a microscope for what a function looks like near a bad point.
  5. §74–81: The microscope's \(\tfrac{1}{z-z_0}\) coefficient — the residue — is the only part that survives integration, and the residue theorem computes any such integral as \(2\pi i\sum\)(residues inside).
Every tool is a generalization of the previous one. Keep that ladder in mind and nothing here is arbitrary.

Part 1 — §48Antiderivatives ↑ top

In real calculus, the Fundamental Theorem says \(\int_a^b f=F(b)-F(a)\) when \(F'=f\). The complex version is the same idea, but the conditions for an antiderivative to exist become interesting — and their failure is where all the famous results live.

Definition \(F\) is an antiderivative of \(f\) on a domain \(D\) if \(F'(z)=f(z)\) at every point of \(D\). Since \(F\) has a complex derivative throughout \(D\), \(F\) is automatically analytic there — and an antiderivative, when it exists, is unique up to an added constant.
The three-way equivalence (the §48 theorem) For \(f\) continuous on a domain \(D\), these are all true or all false together:
  1. \(f\) has an antiderivative \(F\) on \(D\);
  2. integrals between any two fixed points of \(D\) are path-independent: \(\displaystyle\int_{z_1}^{z_2}f\,dz=F(z_2)-F(z_1)\);
  3. \(\displaystyle\oint_C f\,dz=0\) for every closed contour \(C\) lying in \(D\).
Read it as a diagnostic in both directions: find one closed loop with a nonzero integral, and you've proved no antiderivative exists on any domain containing that loop.

Worked (the easy case). \(\displaystyle\int_0^{1+i}z^2\,dz\): \(z^2\) is entire with antiderivative \(z^3/3\), so the path doesn't matter: \(\tfrac{(1+i)^3}{3}=\tfrac{-2+2i}{3}=\tfrac23(-1+i)\). One line — no parametrization.

The \(1/z\) vs. \(1/z^2\) story — the single most important example in §48 Both functions are singular at \(0\), so on the unit circle neither satisfies Cauchy–Goursat (which needs analyticity inside the loop too). Yet: \[\oint_{|z|=1}\frac{dz}{z^2}=0\qquad\text{but}\qquad \oint_{|z|=1}\frac{dz}{z}=2\pi i.\] Why the difference? The antiderivative theorem only needs an antiderivative on a domain containing the curve — the interior is irrelevant. On the punctured plane \(z\neq0\):
  • \(1/z^2\) has the single-valued antiderivative \(-1/z\). It returns to its starting value after a loop, so the integral is \(0\).
  • \(1/z\) would need \(\log z=\ln|z|+i\arg z\) — but \(\arg z\) grows by \(2\pi\) per loop and never returns. No single-valued antiderivative exists on any domain encircling \(0\), and the loop integral picks up exactly that argument jump: \(i\cdot2\pi\).
This is the \(n=-1\) exception in \(\oint_C(z-z_0)^{n}\,dz\): every integer power except \(-1\) antidifferentiates cleanly (single-valued \(\tfrac{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}{n+1}\)) and loops to \(0\); only \((z-z_0)^{-1}\) winds.
Test: does \(f\) have an antiderivative here?
  1. Analytic everywhere on a hole-free domain? Yes — always. Polynomials, \(e^z\), \(\sin z\), \(\cosh z\): antiderivatives exist, integrate by endpoints.
  2. Singularities present, and your path loops around one? Check the residue (the \(\tfrac{1}{z-z_0}\) coefficient of the Laurent series — Part 5). Nonzero residue ⟹ no single-valued antiderivative. Residue zero at every enclosed point ⟹ an antiderivative exists despite the singularities (that's \(1/z^2\)).
  3. Branch point enclosed (\(\log z\), \(z^{1/2}\), any non-integer power)? ⟹ no single-valued antiderivative on a loop around it.
Eyeball version: the only obstructions are a simple-pole term \(\tfrac{1}{z-z_0}\) and branch points. Everything else antidifferentiates.

Part 2 — §50–§53The Cauchy–Goursat Theorem & Deformation ↑ top

§48 needs you to find an antiderivative. Cauchy–Goursat removes that chore: analyticity alone is enough.

Cauchy–Goursat theorem (§50) If \(f\) is analytic at every point inside and on a simple closed contour \(C\), then \[\oint_C f(z)\,dz=0.\] Both halves of the hypothesis matter: the curve and its whole interior. A singularity anywhere inside — even one the curve never touches — voids the theorem.

Where it comes from. Split \(f=u+iv\), \(dz=dx+i\,dy\); the contour integral becomes two real line integrals, and Green's theorem converts each into a double integral over the enclosed region: \[\oint_C f\,dz=\iint_R(-v_x-u_y)\,dA\;+\;i\iint_R(u_x-v_y)\,dA.\] Now look at the integrands: by the Cauchy–Riemann equations both are identically zero. So the integral is zero — analyticity kills it pointwise, before any integration happens. (Cauchy's original proof needed \(f'\) continuous; Goursat showed analyticity alone suffices, which is why the modern statement is so clean.)

Upgrades: simply & multiply connected domains (§52–53) A simply connected domain has no holes. There, \(\oint_C f\,dz=0\) for every closed contour (even self-crossing ones — they decompose into simple loops). A multiply connected domain has holes, and the right tool becomes the

Principle of deformation of paths: if \(C_1\) can be continuously deformed into \(C_2\) without ever crossing a point where \(f\) fails to be analytic, then \[\oint_{C_1}f\,dz=\oint_{C_2}f\,dz.\] This is how every big-contour problem gets reduced: shrink the contour down to a tiny circle hugging each singularity. The value of the integral only "sees" the singularities, not the shape of the curve.

Worked (deformation in action). Evaluate \(\oint_C\frac{dz}{z-(2+i)}\) around the rectangle \(0\le x\le3,\ 0\le y\le2\). The point \(2+i\) is inside. The integrand is analytic everywhere else, so deform the rectangle to a small circle centered at \(2+i\): the integral equals the circle's value, \(2\pi i\). No parametrization of the rectangle needed — that's the entire point of deformation.

Trap — “analytic on the curve” is not enough \(\oint_{|z|=1}\frac{dz}{z}\): the integrand is perfectly analytic on the unit circle (the only bad point is \(0\), which the curve avoids). Cauchy–Goursat still does not apply, because \(0\) is inside. Students lose points by checking the curve and forgetting the interior. The checklist is always: find every singularity, then ask "is it inside?"
Aside your professor mentioned: the area formula For a positively oriented simple closed contour \(C\), the Green's-theorem identity applied to \(f(z)=\bar z\) (which is nowhere analytic, so Cauchy–Goursat does not zero it out) gives \[\text{Area enclosed by }C=\frac{1}{2i}\oint_C \bar z\,dz.\] A line integral around the boundary measures the area inside — the complex cousin of the shoelace formula. (This is homework p. 170–172 #7.)

Part 3 — §54–§55The Cauchy Integral Formula ↑ top

Cauchy–Goursat handles loops with no singularities inside. The integral formula handles the next case: exactly one bad point inside, of the special form \(\tfrac{1}{z-z_0}\). And it says something astonishing along the way.

Cauchy integral formula (§54) Let \(f\) be analytic inside and on a positively oriented simple closed contour \(C\), with \(z_0\) interior to \(C\). Then \[\oint_C\frac{f(z)}{z-z_0}\,dz=2\pi i\,f(z_0).\] Read right-to-left it's even more striking: \(f(z_0)=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_C\frac{f(z)}{z-z_0}dz\) — the boundary values of an analytic function completely determine its value at every interior point. Analytic functions are that rigid.
Extension to derivatives (§55) \[\oint_C\frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz=\frac{2\pi i}{n!}\,f^{(n)}(z_0).\] Watch the off-by-one: denominator power \(n+1\) ⟹ you take the \(n\)th derivative. A \((z-z_0)^3\) downstairs means \(n=2\) and \(\tfrac{2\pi i}{2!}f''(z_0)\).

Free consequence: since the right side exists for every \(n\), an analytic function automatically has derivatives of all orders, all analytic. Once complex-differentiable ⟹ infinitely differentiable. Nothing like this is true in real calculus, and it's why Taylor series (Part 4) always work for analytic functions.

Why it's true, in one breath: deform \(C\) to a tiny circle of radius \(\rho\) around \(z_0\) (deformation principle). On that circle \(\oint\frac{dz}{z-z_0}=2\pi i\) exactly, and the leftover error term \(\oint\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}dz\) is killed by the ML inequality: continuity makes \(|f(z)-f(z_0)|<\varepsilon\) on a small enough circle, so the error is at most \(\tfrac{\varepsilon}{\rho}\cdot2\pi\rho=2\pi\varepsilon\to0\). Your first-half ML skill is literally the engine of this proof.

The skill Forcing an integrand into the right form

The formula only fires when the integrand looks like \(\frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{k}}\) with \(f\) analytic inside. Real problems never arrive that way — the exam skill is the algebra that gets them there. The maneuvers:

The five maneuvers
  1. Factor the denominator completely and locate every root.
  2. Sort roots inside vs. outside \(C\) (compare each \(|z_k|\) — or distance to the contour's center — against the radius). Outside roots are harmless: fold their factors into \(f\). Inside roots are the action.
  3. Normalize the leading coefficient: the formula needs the factor as exactly \((z-z_0)\), so rewrite \(2z+1=2\left(z+\tfrac12\right)\) and pull the \(\tfrac12\) out front. Forgetting this scales your answer — the most common error on this topic.
  4. Repeated inside factor \((z-z_0)^{n+1}\)? Use the derivative extension with the matching \(n\).
  5. Two or more inside roots? Partial fractions to split into one-pole pieces (or jump to the residue theorem, Part 5, which automates exactly this).

Worked — all maneuvers at once. \(\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=2}\frac{z^2+1}{z(2z-1)(z-3)}\,dz.\)
Factor & sort: roots \(0,\ \tfrac12,\ 3\); the first two are inside \(|z|=2\), and \(3\) is outside (it contributes nothing).
Normalize: \(2z-1=2(z-\tfrac12)\), giving a global factor \(\tfrac12\).
Two inside poles — handle each: at \(z=0\), strip the \(z\) factor and evaluate the rest: \(\frac{0+1}{(-\frac12)(-3)}=\frac23\). At \(z=\tfrac12\), strip \((z-\tfrac12)\): \(\frac{\frac14+1}{\frac12(\frac12-3)}=\frac{5/4}{-5/4}=-1\).
Assemble: \(\tfrac12\cdot2\pi i\left[\tfrac23-1\right]=\pi i\left(-\tfrac13\right)=-\dfrac{\pi i}{3}.\)
The formula was the last 10%; the form-forcing was the problem.

Worked — derivative extension. \(\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=1}\frac{e^{2z}}{z^4}\,dz\): denominator power \(4=n+1\Rightarrow n=3\), \(f=e^{2z}\), \(f'''(z)=8e^{2z}\), so the value is \(\frac{2\pi i}{3!}\cdot 8=\frac{8\pi i}{3}.\)

Consequences worth knowing (§57–58, light touch) From the derivative formula plus the ML inequality: Liouville's theorem — a bounded entire function is constant — and from it the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra (every nonconstant polynomial has a root: if \(P\) had none, \(1/P\) would be bounded and entire, hence constant — contradiction). These aren't in your assigned problem sets, but they show why the integral formula is considered the heart of the subject.

Part 4 — §62–§64Taylor Series ↑ top

Series are the microscope: they describe a function near a point as a sum of powers. For analytic functions the microscope always works — that's Taylor's theorem — and the infinitely-many-derivatives fact from Part 3 is exactly what makes it possible.

Taylor's theorem If \(f\) is analytic throughout an open disk \(|z-z_0|<R_0\), then at every point of that disk \[f(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_n(z-z_0)^n,\qquad a_n=\frac{f^{(n)}(z_0)}{n!}.\] With \(z_0=0\) it's called a Maclaurin series. Radius of convergence = the distance from \(z_0\) to the nearest singularity of \(f\). That one sentence answers every "largest circle of convergence" question instantly — no ratio tests needed.
The standard kit (memorize these six) \[e^z=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{z^n}{n!},\qquad\qquad \frac{1}{1-z}=\sum_{n=0}^\infty z^n\ \ (|z|<1),\] \[\sin z=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(-1)^n z^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!},\qquad \cos z=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(-1)^n z^{2n}}{(2n)!},\] \[\sinh z=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{z^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!},\qquad \cosh z=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{z^{2n}}{(2n)!}.\] The entire ones hold for all \(z\); the geometric series needs \(|z|<1\) — and it is the workhorse of Part 5.
The technique: never differentiate, always manipulate Computing \(f^{(n)}(z_0)\) by hand is the slow, error-prone route. Instead build new series from the kit:
  • Substitute: \(\cosh(z^2)\) is the \(\cosh\) series with \(z\to z^2\). \(\frac{1}{1+w}\) is the geometric series with \(z\to-w\).
  • Multiply by powers: \(z\cosh(z^2)=\sum \tfrac{z^{4n+1}}{(2n)!}\).
  • Shift the center: for a series about \(z_0\neq0\), set \(w=z-z_0\), rewrite \(f\) in terms of \(w\) (often via an identity like \(e^z=e\cdot e^{z-1}\) or \(\sinh(w+\pi i)=-\sinh w\)), expand in \(w\).
  • Read coefficients backwards: uniqueness (Part 4½ below) means the coefficient of \(z^k\) in any valid expansion is \(f^{(k)}(0)/k!\) — so missing powers prove derivatives vanish. E.g. \(\sin(z^2)=\sum\tfrac{(-1)^nz^{4n+2}}{(2n+1)!}\) contains only powers \(\equiv2\pmod 4\), which instantly gives \(f^{(4n)}(0)=0\) and \(f^{(2n+1)}(0)=0\).

Worked — largest circle. Maclaurin series of \(\tanh z=\sinh z/\cosh z\): the nearest singularities to \(0\) are the zeros of \(\cosh z\) at \(z=\pm\tfrac{\pi}{2}i\), distance \(\tfrac\pi2\). So the series converges exactly on \(|z|<\tfrac\pi2\), and dividing the two kit series gives \(\tanh z=z-\tfrac{z^3}{3}+\cdots\). The radius came from geometry, not from any convergence test.

§71–72: term-by-term operations & uniqueness Inside its circle (or annulus) of convergence, a power series may be differentiated and integrated term by term, and the resulting series converge in the same region. And representations are unique: if you produce any series for \(f\) in a region by any valid trick, it is the Taylor/Laurent series there — the coefficients must match \(f^{(n)}(z_0)/n!\). This is the license behind every "manipulate, don't differentiate" move, and behind removable-singularity arguments: \(\frac{1-\cos z}{z^2}=\frac{1}{2!}-\frac{z^2}{4!}+\cdots\) has no negative powers, so defining the value \(\tfrac12\) at \(0\) makes the function entire.

Part 5 — §66–§68Laurent Series ↑ top

Taylor series need a disk where \(f\) is analytic — useless near a singularity. Laurent's theorem is the fix: in any annulus where \(f\) is analytic, it expands into powers of \((z-z_0)\) that include negative exponents. Those negative powers are precisely the record of how \(f\) misbehaves at \(z_0\).

Laurent's theorem If \(f\) is analytic throughout the annulus \(R_1<|z-z_0|<R_2\), then everywhere in it \[f(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_n(z-z_0)^n\;+\;\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{b_n}{(z-z_0)^n}.\] The negative-power tail is the principal part. The coefficient \(b_1\) — of \(\tfrac{1}{z-z_0}\) — is the residue, the single number Part 6 is built on. Like Taylor series, the representation in a given annulus is unique.
How to actually find Laurent series (never use coefficient integrals) Everything reduces to the geometric series \(\frac{1}{1-w}=\sum w^n\), valid when \(|w|<1\) — the entire skill is choosing \(w\) so it's small in the region you're asked about.
  1. Partial fractions first if the denominator has several factors.
  2. Near a point (small \(|z-z_0|\)): arrange each piece as \(\frac{1}{1-(\text{small})}\) and expand in positive powers.
  3. Far away (large \(|z|\)): factor the biggest term out of the denominator so the ratio is \(\tfrac{1}{z}\)-sized: \(\frac{1}{z-a}=\frac1z\cdot\frac{1}{1-a/z}=\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{a^{n-1}}{z^{n}}\) for \(|z|>|a|\). Negative powers come out automatically.
  4. Off-center expansions: substitute \(w=z-z_0\) everywhere first, then play the same game in \(w\).
  5. Singularities cut the plane into annuli — a function with bad points at \(|z|=1\) and \(|z|=2\) has three different expansions in powers of \(z\) (\(|z|<1\), \(1<|z|<2\), \(|z|>2\)), one per region. Which form of the geometric series you use for each factor depends on the region.

Worked — same function, two regions. \(f(z)=\dfrac{1}{z^2(1-z)}\).
In \(0<|z|<1\): \(\tfrac{1}{1-z}=\sum z^n\), so \(f=\tfrac{1}{z^2}\sum z^n=\dfrac{1}{z^2}+\dfrac1z+1+z+\cdots\) — principal part \(\tfrac{1}{z^2}+\tfrac1z\), residue \(b_1=1\).
In \(|z|>1\): \(\tfrac{1}{1-z}=-\tfrac1z\cdot\tfrac{1}{1-1/z}=-\sum_{n=1}^\infty z^{-n}\), so \(f=-\sum_{n=3}^{\infty}\dfrac{1}{z^{n}}\) — all negative powers, and notice no \(\tfrac1z\) term here. Same function, different annulus, different series.

Worked — substitution beats everything. \(z^2\sin(1/z^2)\) on \(0<|z|<\infty\): drop \(w=1/z^2\) into the sine series: \(z^2\sum\frac{(-1)^n}{(2n+1)!}z^{-(4n+2)}=1-\dfrac{1}{3!\,z^{4}}+\dfrac{1}{5!\,z^{8}}-\cdots\) — infinitely many negative powers (an essential singularity, Part 6), found in one line.

Part 6 — §74–§81Residues and Poles ↑ top

Here's the payoff of the whole course. Integrate the Laurent series of \(f\) term by term around a small loop at \(z_0\): every power \((z-z_0)^k\) with \(k\neq-1\) has a single-valued antiderivative and integrates to \(0\). Only the \(\tfrac{b_1}{z-z_0}\) term survives, contributing \(2\pi i\,b_1\). So the residue is the only part of a function that integration can see.

Residue & the residue theorem (§75–76) At an isolated singular point \(z_0\), \(\operatorname*{Res}_{z=z_0}f=b_1\) (the \(\tfrac{1}{z-z_0}\) Laurent coefficient). If \(f\) is analytic inside and on a positively oriented simple closed contour \(C\) except at finitely many points \(z_k\) inside, then \[\oint_C f(z)\,dz=2\pi i\sum_{k}\operatorname*{Res}_{z=z_k}f(z).\] Everything earlier is a special case: no singularities ⟹ sum is empty ⟹ Cauchy–Goursat. One simple pole of the form \(\tfrac{f}{z-z_0}\) ⟹ residue is \(f(z_0)\) ⟹ the Cauchy integral formula.
The three types of isolated singularity (§78) Classify by the principal part at \(z_0\):
TypePrincipal partBehavior / example
Removableempty (all \(b_n=0\))fixable by defining one value; residue \(0\). \(\tfrac{\sin z}{z}\) at \(0\).
Pole of order \(m\)stops at \(\tfrac{b_m}{(z-z_0)^m},\ b_m\neq0\)\(|f|\to\infty\). \(m=1\): simple pole. \(\tfrac{\cos z}{z}\) at \(0\) (\(m=1\)).
Essentialinfinitely many \(b_n\neq0\)wild behavior; only route to the residue is the series itself. \(e^{1/z}\) at \(0\).
Computing residues — the toolbox, fastest first
  1. Simple pole, factored form \(\tfrac{\phi(z)}{z-z_0}\), \(\phi\) analytic: \(\operatorname{Res}=\phi(z_0)\). Equivalently \(\operatorname{Res}=\lim_{z\to z_0}(z-z_0)f(z)\).
  2. Simple pole, \(p/q\) form where \(q\) has a simple zero at \(z_0\): \(\operatorname{Res}=\dfrac{p(z_0)}{q'(z_0)}\). Fastest when factoring is ugly (e.g. \(q=z^4+1\)).
  3. Pole of order \(m\): write \(f=\dfrac{\phi(z)}{(z-z_0)^m}\) with \(\phi\) analytic, \(\phi(z_0)\neq0\); then \(\operatorname{Res}=\dfrac{\phi^{(m-1)}(z_0)}{(m-1)!}\). (For \(m=1\) this collapses to tool 1.)
  4. Essential singularity (or any doubt): expand the Laurent series and read \(b_1\) directly. For products like \(z^2e^{1/z}\), multiply series and hunt the \(\tfrac1z\) term.

Worked — order matters. \(\operatorname*{Res}_{z=0}\dfrac{\sinh z}{z^4}\): naive guess says pole of order 4, but \(\sinh z=z+\tfrac{z^3}{3!}+\cdots\) cancels one power: \(\tfrac{\sinh z}{z^4}=\tfrac{1}{z^3}+\tfrac{1}{6z}+\cdots\). True order \(m=3\), residue \(\tfrac16\) — read straight off the series, no derivatives. Lesson: when numerator and denominator both vanish, the series is faster and safer than the \(\phi^{(m-1)}\) formula.

Residue at infinity (§77) — the one-residue shortcut When a contour encloses many singularities, trade them all for a single computation at the origin: \[\oint_C f(z)\,dz=2\pi i\,\operatorname*{Res}_{z=0}\!\left[\frac{1}{z^2}\,f\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)\right].\] Substitute, simplify, find one residue at \(0\) — done. Worth reaching for whenever three or more poles sit inside \(C\) (e.g. \(\oint_{|z|=3}\frac{(3z+2)^2}{z(z-1)(2z+5)}dz\): one residue instead of three).
Master method for \(\oint_C f\,dz\)
  1. Find all singularities (factor; look for zeros of denominators, branch points, essential points).
  2. Keep only those inside \(C\). None inside ⟹ answer \(0\) and stop.
  3. Classify each (removable / pole order \(m\) / essential) and compute its residue with the cheapest tool above.
  4. Answer \(=2\pi i\times\)(sum of inside residues). Three-plus poles? Consider the residue at infinity instead.

Part 7 — instructor extrasWinding Numbers & Other Things Professors Mention ↑ top

7.1 The winding number

Every theorem so far assumed a simple closed contour — one loop, counterclockwise. The winding number handles everything else: contours that loop several times, run clockwise, or cross themselves.

Definition & the master formula The winding number \(n(C,z_0)\) is the net number of counterclockwise turns \(C\) makes around \(z_0\): each CCW loop counts \(+1\), each clockwise loop \(-1\), and a point the curve never encloses gets \(0\). It is computed/defined by \[n(C,z_0)=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_C\frac{dz}{z-z_0},\] and the fully general residue theorem weights every singularity by it: \[\oint_C f\,dz=2\pi i\sum_k n(C,z_k)\,\operatorname*{Res}_{z=z_k}f.\] For a simple positively oriented contour every inside point has \(n=1\) and this collapses to the §76 statement — so the book's version is the winding-number theorem with all the weights set to 1.

Why each loop adds exactly 1: the antiderivative-that-isn't, \(\log(z-z_0)\), has a single-valued real part but its imaginary part \(\arg(z-z_0)\) climbs by \(2\pi\) per counterclockwise trip. The integral records the total climb: \(k\) loops ⟹ argument gain \(2\pi k\) ⟹ integral \(2\pi i k\). The winding number is just "total angle swept, in units of full turns."

Worked. \(C\): the unit circle traversed twice CCW (parametrized \(z=e^{i\theta},\ 0\le\theta\le4\pi\) — the \(4\pi\) is the tell). Then \(\displaystyle\oint_C\frac{e^z}{z}\,dz=2\pi i\cdot n(C,0)\cdot\operatorname*{Res}_{z=0}\frac{e^z}{z}=2\pi i\cdot2\cdot1=4\pi i.\) Clockwise once instead? \(n=-1\), answer \(-2\pi i\). Point outside? \(n=0\), contributes nothing — same as always.

Recognition on an exam Reach for winding numbers only when the problem says so: "traversed twice," a \(\theta\)-range longer than \(2\pi\), "clockwise"/negative orientation, or a drawn self-crossing curve. A plain "positively oriented circle" is \(n=1\) — don't overcomplicate it.

7.2 Quick-recognition: does an antiderivative exist?

The residue test, one more time On a domain that loops around a singularity, \(f\) has a single-valued antiderivative iff the residue there is zero (and no branch point is enclosed). Nonzero residue ⟹ some loop integral is \(2\pi i\,b_1\neq0\) ⟹ no antiderivative. This unifies the \(1/z\) story, the winding number, and the residue theorem into one sentence: the residue is the obstruction.

7.3 The area formula

Area as a contour integral \(\displaystyle\text{Area enclosed by }C=\frac{1}{2i}\oint_C\bar z\,dz\) for a positively oriented simple closed \(C\). It works because \(\bar z\) is nowhere analytic — Green's theorem applies but Cauchy–Goursat can't zero it out. A favorite "explain why this doesn't contradict Cauchy–Goursat" question.

Laurent (§66–68) Annulus ⟹ positive and negative powers; one expansion per annular region. Geometric series with \(|w|<1\): near ⟹ positive powers; far ⟹ factor out \(z\), negative powers. \(b_1\)= residue.
Singularities (§78) Principal part empty ⟹ removable (Res \(=0\)); finite ⟹ pole of order \(m\); infinite ⟹ essential (series only).
Residues (§75–80) Simple: \(\lim(z-z_0)f\) or \(p(z_0)/q'(z_0)\). Order \(m\): \(\frac{\phi^{(m-1)}(z_0)}{(m-1)!}\), \(\phi=(z-z_0)^mf\). Cancellation? Trust the series, not the formula.
Residue theorem (§76–77) + winding \(\oint_C f=2\pi i\sum n(C,z_k)\operatorname{Res}_{z_k}f\) (simple CCW contour: all \(n=1\)). Many poles: \(=2\pi i\operatorname{Res}_{0}[\tfrac{1}{z^2}f(1/z)]\).
★ The master decision tree for \(\oint_C f\,dz\)
  1. Singularities inside \(C\)? None ⟹ \(0\) (Cauchy–Goursat).
  2. One, of the shape \(\tfrac{g}{(z-z_0)^{k}}\) with \(g\) analytic inside ⟹ integral formula (normalize coefficients; \(k=n+1\)).
  3. Several ⟹ residue theorem: classify, compute each residue cheaply, sum, \(\times2\pi i\). Three+ ⟹ consider residue at infinity.
  4. Contour loops multiple times / clockwise ⟹ weight each residue by its winding number.
  5. Open path, integrand analytic with antiderivative ⟹ endpoints only.
